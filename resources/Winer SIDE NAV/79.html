Messier 79

M79: M79 is one of the few globular clusters visible in the winter. With a declination of 24 degrees south during the winter, this cluster appears low in the winter sky. M79 is too low below the horizon at latitudes above 40 degrees north, however, can be viewed between the 25-30 degrees latitude north. M79 is highest and most easily observed when Orion is “standing” straight up. Use Orion’s feet to guide you, star hop down to the two brightest stars in Lepus, to locate the globular cluster, extend a line from Arned to Nihal by doubling their separation.

Fun Facts: This globular cluster was discovered by P Mechain in 1780. Unlike most globular clusters that are in a halo around the galactic core, M79 lies far outside of it. According to some scholarly articles, the distance of M79 is 45000 light years and has a diameter of 80 light years. It’s estimated to be 400,000 times solar masses and it contains up to 7 variable stars.

Messiers Comments: December 17, 1780
`Nebula without star, situated below Lepus, and on the same parallel as a star of sixth magnitude: seen by M. Méchain on October 26, 1780. M. Messier looked for it on the following December 17: this nebula is beautiful; the center brilliant, the nebulosity a little diffuse; its position was determined from the star Epsilon Leporis, of fourth magnitude.'
