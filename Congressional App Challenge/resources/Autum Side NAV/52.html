Messier 52

M52 is a dense open cluster contiaing over 200 stars. Though most appar white, keep observing for red orange and blue stars nearby. These brigher stars are not members of the cluster.  Astrophotographers are familiar with the clusteres location as it ies beside the bubble nebulae which is one of the easiest target to be picked up by cameras, but a challenge for the eye. The cluster is often known as the salt and pepper cluster. To ocate the luser,, double the line connecting Shedar to Caph, which are the two end stars of Cassiopeia. When observing M52 as a part of a March  Marathon, the cluster will be located belwo Cassiopeia, just below the horizon in the north west.

Messiers Comments on M52: `Cluster of very small [faint] stars, mingled with nebulosity, which can be seen only with an achromatic telescope. It was when he observed the Comet which appeared in this year that M. Messier saw this cluster, which was close to the comet on the 7th of September 1774; it is below the star d Cassiopeiae: that star was used to determine [the position of] both the cluster of stars and the comet.'

Fun Facts: This open cluster was discovered by Messier in 1774. According to Kharchenko et al. (2005), the distance of M52 is 4630 light years and its diameter is 22 light years. It contains 6000 stars and its estimated age is 25-165 million years.
